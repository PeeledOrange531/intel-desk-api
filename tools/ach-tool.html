Analysis of Competing Hypotheses

📖 How to use ACH — CIA Structured Analytical Technique Richards J. Heuer Jr. · Psychology of Intelligence Analysis (1999)
▾
1
Hypotheses
List all plausible explanations. Do not dismiss any possibility early — ACH requires you to challenge your own assumptions.
2
Evidence
List every piece of evidence, assumption, or argument as a single factual statement. Include things that seem obvious.
3
Score the matrix
For each evidence × hypothesis pair, ask: "If this hypothesis were true, would I expect to see this evidence?" Score accordingly.
4
Read the results
The hypothesis with the lowest inconsistency score best fits the evidence. Focus on eliminating hypotheses, not confirming one.
5
Check diagnostics
Diagnostic evidence items discriminate between hypotheses — these are your most valuable pieces of evidence.
6
Revisit & iterate
ACH is not a one-time exercise. As new evidence emerges, update the matrix and re-examine your conclusions.
Why ACH matters
Human analysts naturally seek evidence that confirms their existing beliefs — a cognitive bias called confirmation bias. ACH inverts this: instead of building a case for your preferred hypothesis, you systematically attempt to disprove each one. The hypothesis that cannot be eliminated by the evidence is the one that best fits reality. This technique was developed by Richards J. Heuer Jr. at the CIA and is now standard practice in intelligence analysis, law enforcement, and competitive intelligence worldwide.
Source: Heuer, R.J. (1999). Psychology of Intelligence Analysis. CIA Center for the Study of Intelligence. Chapter 8.
